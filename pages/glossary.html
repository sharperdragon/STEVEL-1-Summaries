Important Terms
AnictericWithout jaundice; often used in viral hepatitis questions to imply normal bilirubin.
AcalculousWithout stones; commonly seen in acalculous cholecystitis (e.g., ICU patient).
DesmoplasiaFibrous tissue formation in response to neoplasm; often in pancreatic cancer.
KoilocyteEpithelial cell with perinuclear halo and nuclear wrinkling; seen in HPV infection.
Dystrophic CalcificationCalcium deposition in necrotic tissue despite normal serum Ca²⁺ levels.
Metastatic CalcificationCalcium deposition in normal tissues due to hypercalcemia (e.g., hyperparathyroidism).
Oncotic PressureOsmotic pressure due to plasma proteins (mainly albumin); affects edema formation.
Spindle CellsElongated cells seen in mesenchymal tumors (e.g., sarcomas).
PoikilocytosisRBCs of varying shapes; indicates bone marrow stress or chronic anemia.
SyncytiumMultinucleated cell mass from cell fusion; seen in viral infections (e.g., RSV).
MyeloperoxidaseEnzyme in neutrophils; generates bleach (HOCl) to kill microbes; stains blue-green.
HamartomaBenign, disorganized tissue growth native to the site (e.g., lung hamartoma).
ChoristomaNormal tissue in an abnormal location (e.g., gastric mucosa in Meckel diverticulum).
HaptenSmall molecule that becomes immunogenic only when attached to a carrier protein.
SuperantigenActivates many T cells nonspecifically by binding MHC II and TCR outside antigen site.
PyknosisNuclear condensation seen in apoptosis.
KaryorrhexisNuclear fragmentation seen in cell death.
KaryolysisNuclear fading due to DNA degradation; final stage of nuclear destruction.
ClubbingBulbous enlargement of distal fingers; associated with chronic hypoxia.
MonoclonalOriginating from a single cell; key concept in neoplasia (e.g., monoclonal Ig spike in myeloma).
PolyclonalOriginating from many cells; suggests reactive or benign process.
DesquamationPeeling or shedding of skin layers; seen in staph scalded skin syndrome, toxic shock, or Kawasaki.
TelangiectasiaDilated superficial blood vessels; seen in Osler-Weber-Rendu and other syndromes.
AnaplasiaLoss of cell differentiation and function; marker of malignancy.
Chediak-HigashiDefect in lysosomal trafficking; giant granules in neutrophils; impaired phagocytosis.
OpsoninMolecule (e.g., IgG or C3b) that enhances phagocytosis by tagging pathogens.
Punched-Out UlcerWell-demarcated lesion with sharp edges; seen in syphilis, diabetes, or TB skin lesions.
Genetics
Dominant Negative MutationMutant protein interferes with normal protein (e.g., collagen in osteogenesis imperfecta).
FISHUses fluorescent probes to localize genes or chromosomal abnormalities.
PromoterDNA sequence where RNA polymerase binds to initiate transcription.
EnhancerRegulatory DNA that increases gene transcription from a distance.
PleiotropyOne gene affects multiple phenotypic traits (e.g., PKU).
Locus HeterogeneityMutations at different loci cause the same phenotype (e.g., albinism).
Allelic HeterogeneityDifferent mutations at the same locus produce similar phenotype (e.g., β-thalassemia).
Variable ExpressivitySame genotype, different phenotypes (e.g., NF1).
Incomplete PenetranceNot all individuals with mutation show phenotype.
NondisjunctionFailure of homologous chromosomes or sister chromatids to separate (e.g., trisomy 21).
TranslocationChromosomal segments exchange (balanced vs. unbalanced; e.g., t(9;22) in CML).
DeletionLoss of genetic material (e.g., 22q11 in DiGeorge).
DuplicationExtra copy of a chromosome segment.
InversionSegment flipped within chromosome.
Founder EffectReduced genetic diversity when a population descends from a small ancestral group.
Genetic DriftRandom fluctuations in allele frequencies, more pronounced in small populations.
HaploinsufficiencyOne functional copy of a gene is not enough for normal function (e.g., PAX genes).
ImprintingEpigenetic silencing of one parental allele; only one allele expressed (e.g., Prader-Willi, Angelman).
Trinucleotide Repeat ExpansionRepeats increase with each generation; causes anticipation (e.g., CAG in Huntington).
Chromosomal InstabilityIncreased tendency to acquire chromosomal breaks or rearrangements (e.g., Fanconi anemia).
Skewed X-InactivationPreferential inactivation of one X chromosome; may lead to expression of X-linked disorders in females.
Position EffectGene expression altered by relocation near heterochromatin or regulatory elements.
Somatic MutationMutation acquired after fertilization; affects only a portion of cells/tissues.
Germline MutationMutation in gametes; passed to offspring, affects all cells in progeny.
EpistasisOne gene masks or modifies the effect of another gene.
PenetranceProportion of individuals with a mutation who express the phenotype.
ExpressivityDegree or range of phenotype expression among individuals with the same genotype.
HeteroplasmyPresence of both normal and mutated mitochondrial DNA within a cell.
HomoplasmyAll mitochondrial DNA copies are identical, either normal or mutated.
ChimerismPresence of genetically distinct cells from different zygotes in one person (e.g., bone marrow transplant).
LyonizationAnother term for random X-inactivation in females.
Gene ConversionNon-reciprocal transfer of genetic material between homologous sequences.
Balanced TranslocationChromosomal segments exchange without gain or loss of genetic material; may still cause disease via disruption.
Unbalanced TranslocationChromosomal rearrangement resulting in extra or missing genetic material.
Neurology
AphasiaLanguage impairment due to cortical damage; Broca = non-fluent, Wernicke = fluent but nonsensical.
ApraxiaInability to perform learned motor tasks despite normal strength (e.g., dressing apraxia).
AgnosiaInability to recognize objects, people, or sounds despite intact sensory modalities.
AtaxiaLoss of coordination; cerebellar if ipsilateral, sensory if improved with visual input.
MyoclonusSudden, brief involuntary muscle jerk (e.g., juvenile myoclonic epilepsy).
Decerebrate PosturingExtension of limbs due to brainstem damage below the red nucleus (midbrain/pons).
Decorticate PosturingFlexion of upper limbs due to damage above the red nucleus (cortex/internal capsule).
HemineglectUnawareness of one side of space, usually from right parietal lobe damage.
HyperreflexiaIncreased deep tendon reflexes, indicates upper motor neuron lesion.
HyporeflexiaDecreased or absent reflexes, indicates lower motor neuron lesion.
SpasticityVelocity-dependent increase in muscle tone, seen in UMN lesions.
Flaccid ParalysisLimp, weak muscles with no tone or reflexes; LMN lesion or anterior horn disease.
Babinski SignUpgoing big toe on plantar stimulation; abnormal in adults, normal in infants.
ClonusRhythmic, involuntary contractions from sustained stretch; UMN lesion sign.
Intention TremorOccurs during purposeful movement; cerebellar lesion sign.
Resting TremorOccurs at rest, improves with movement (e.g., Parkinson's).
Essential TremorAction tremor, familial, improves with alcohol.
DysdiadochokinesiaInability to perform rapid alternating movements; cerebellar dysfunction.
NystagmusRepetitive eye movement; central (brainstem) vs peripheral (vestibular) causes.
AnosmiaLoss of smell; can follow trauma to olfactory nerve or frontal lobe injury.
AreflexiaAbsence of reflexes; seen in peripheral neuropathies or acute spinal injury.
SeizureAbnormal, synchronous brain activity; can be focal or generalized.
Status EpilepticusSeizure lasting >5 minutes or recurrent seizures without recovery; emergency.
DiplopiaDouble vision; often due to cranial nerve palsy or extraocular muscle dysfunction.
VertigoFalse sense of motion/spinning; central or peripheral origin.
DysarthriaSlurred or slow speech due to motor dysfunction of speech muscles.
DysphagiaDifficulty swallowing; may be oropharyngeal or esophageal.
DysmetriaImpaired coordination of movement; overshooting or undershooting a target.
Scissoring GaitThighs cross over each other with each step; sign of spastic cerebral palsy.
Steppage GaitHigh stepping due to foot drop; commonly seen in peripheral neuropathy.
Festinating GaitShuffling with short accelerating steps; seen in Parkinson's.
Romberg SignLoss of balance when eyes are closed; indicates sensory ataxia.
Pronator DriftArm drifts downward and pronates with eyes closed; sign of UMN lesion.
HypertoniaIncreased muscle tone; suggests UMN pathology.
HypotoniaDecreased muscle tone; seen in LMN lesions or cerebellar disorders.
GraphesthesiaAbility to identify numbers or letters traced on skin; tests cortical function.
StereognosisAbility to recognize objects by touch; requires intact sensory cortex.
Asterixis"Flapping" tremor of hands when extended; associated with hepatic encephalopathy.
RigidityIncreased resistance throughout movement; lead-pipe or cogwheel types.
AkinesiaAbsence or poverty of movement; often seen in parkinsonism.
BradykinesiaSlowness of movement; hallmark of Parkinson’s disease.
FasciculationsVisible, involuntary muscle twitches; sign of LMN lesion.
ChoreaRapid, involuntary, jerky movements; flowing from one body part to another.
BallismusViolent flinging movements of limbs; usually from subthalamic nucleus damage.
AthetosisSlow, writhing movements; often seen in cerebral palsy.
TicStereotyped, repetitive movement or vocalization; may be suppressible.
PalilaliaInvoluntary repetition of words or phrases; seen in Tourette and parkinsonism.
HemiplegiaParalysis of one side of the body.
AllodyniaPain from stimuli that don't normally cause pain (e.g., light touch).
DysesthesiaUnpleasant, abnormal sense of touch; often painful.
Cardio-Pulm
PalpitationsAwareness of heartbeat; described as pounding, fluttering, or irregular.
Dyspnea on Exertion (DOE)Shortness of breath during physical activity; often a sign of cardiac dysfunction.
OrthopneaShortness of breath when lying flat; relieved by sitting up.
Paroxysmal Nocturnal Dyspnea (PND)Sudden nighttime shortness of breath causing patient to wake up gasping.
S3 Heart SoundEarly diastolic sound; associated with volume overload or heart failure.
S4 Heart SoundLate diastolic sound; indicates stiff or hypertrophic ventricle.
Pulse PressureDifference between systolic and diastolic BP; widened in aortic regurgitation, narrowed in shock.
Pulsus ParadoxusExaggerated ↓ in systolic BP during inspiration (>10 mmHg); seen in tamponade, asthma.
Pulsus AlternansAlternating strong and weak pulses; indicates left ventricular dysfunction.
Corrigan PulseBounding and forceful pulse with rapid rise and fall; associated with aortic regurgitation.
Displaced PMIPoint of maximal impulse shifted laterally/inferiorly; suggests LVH or dilation.
MurmurAudible blood flow turbulence; graded I–VI, systolic or diastolic.
Rub (Pericardial Friction Rub)High-pitched scratchy sound; heard best with patient leaning forward, sign of pericarditis.
Heave or LiftVisible or palpable chest wall movement from abnormal heart contraction.
ThrillPalpable vibration over a murmur site; suggests loud murmur (grade ≥4).
Rales (Crackles)Discontinuous popping sounds; heard on inspiration. Fine crackles = fibrosis, coarse crackles = pulmonary edema or pneumonia.
RhonchiLow-pitched, snoring sounds; continuous and heard on expiration. Suggests airway secretions, clears with coughing (e.g., bronchitis).
WheezingHigh-pitched, musical sound; typically expiratory. Indicates airway narrowing (e.g., asthma, COPD).
StridorMonophonic inspiratory sound from upper airway obstruction (e.g., croup, foreign body).
Pleural RubGrating, sandpaper-like sound; heard at end of inspiration/expiration. Due to inflamed pleura rubbing together (e.g., pleuritis).
Bronchial Breath SoundsLouder, high-pitched breath sounds normally heard over trachea; when heard over lung fields, suggest consolidation.
Absent Breath SoundsNo airflow heard; suggests pneumothorax, large effusion, or severe obstruction.
BronchophonyIncreased clarity of spoken words on auscultation; indicates consolidation.
Egophony“E” sounds like “A” over affected area; classic sign of lobar pneumonia.
Whispered PectoriloquyWhispered speech is distinctly heard; indicates lung consolidation or mass effect.
HyperresonanceLow-pitched, booming percussion sound; seen in emphysema, asthma, or pneumothorax.
Dullness to PercussionFlat sound suggesting fluid or mass (e.g., pleural effusion, consolidation, atelectasis).
Hematology
AnisocytosisVariation in red blood cell size; elevated RDW on CBC.
SchistocyteFragmented RBC; hallmark of microangiopathic hemolytic anemia.
SpherocyteRound, hyperchromic RBCs lacking central pallor; seen in hereditary spherocytosis or AIHA.
Tear Drop Cell (Dacrocyte)RBC shaped like a teardrop; seen in myelofibrosis or marrow infiltration.
Target CellRBC with central staining; seen in liver disease, thalassemia, asplenia.
Basophilic StipplingBlue granules in RBC cytoplasm; seen in lead poisoning, sideroblastic anemia.
Howell-Jolly BodyNuclear remnant in RBC; indicates asplenia or hyposplenia.
Heinz BodyDenatured hemoglobin precipitates; seen in G6PD deficiency.
Bite CellRBC with "bitten-out" appearance; macrophages remove Heinz bodies.
Rouleaux FormationRBCs stacked like coins; seen in high plasma protein states (e.g., multiple myeloma).
Hypersegmented NeutrophilNeutrophil with >5 nuclear lobes; classic for B12 or folate deficiency.
Leukemoid ReactionBenign leukocytosis with left shift; often from infection or stress.
Left ShiftIncreased immature neutrophils (bands); sign of acute inflammation or infection.
Dry TapBone marrow aspiration yields no sample; seen in myelofibrosis or marrow infiltration.
PancytopeniaReduction in all blood cell lines: RBCs, WBCs, and platelets.
ThrombocytopeniaLow platelet count; risk of bleeding.
ThrombocytosisElevated platelet count; can be reactive or neoplastic (essential thrombocythemia).
ReticulocytosisIncreased immature RBCs; sign of marrow response to anemia or hemolysis.
Coombs PositivePresence of antibodies on RBCs; indicates autoimmune hemolytic anemia.
HemolysisPremature RBC destruction; leads to ↑ LDH, ↑ bilirubin, ↓ haptoglobin.
HaptoglobinProtein that binds free hemoglobin; decreased in intravascular hemolysis.
FerritinIron storage protein; ↓ in iron deficiency, ↑ in anemia of chronic disease.
TIBC (Total Iron Binding Capacity)Indirect measure of transferrin; ↑ in iron deficiency anemia.
RDW (Red Cell Distribution Width)Measure of RBC size variability; elevated in mixed anemias.
HemosiderinuriaRenal tubular sloughing of iron from filtered hemoglobin; sign of chronic hemolysis.
Erythroid HyperplasiaBone marrow finding with ↑ RBC precursors; seen in hemolysis or chronic anemia.
Teardrop CellDistorted RBCs due to squeezed marrow; seen in myelophthisic processes.
